Misconceptions connected with explicit teaching

Explicit teaching harnesses a range of pedagogical approaches with the aim of developing both deeper learning and understanding in students.

At times, this strategy is perceived to be a teacher-centred approach used solely for the delivery of scripted or sequenced instructions that promote step-by-step procedures, mastery of skills, or concept development. This interpretation of explicit teaching often becomes associated with passive learning, systematic instruction, or rote learning practices.

As you learn more about explicit teaching, you’ll better be able to differentiate it and ensure you don’t fall into these traps.

Helping students to make meaning

Learning without meaning disconnects students from being able to construct their own knowledge and understanding.

In a cognitively stimulating lesson, students have opportunities to develop conceptual understanding, gain skills in higher-order thinking, actively contribute to lessons, and reason mathematically.

Encouraging students to reason and reflect

Questioning plays a really important role in helping teachers gauge students’ understanding. Different questions are used for different purposes.

Read through each question and consider the purpose of the question. Is it aiming to:

  • probe student thinking
  • encouraging reflection or the justification of a response
  • draw out the mathematics
  • lead the student towards understanding, or
  • gather information

Acknowledging that there may be no one correct answer, select a purpose for each question that's the most appropriate.

Modelling mathematics

Modelling of mathematical concepts is a critically important part of the learning journey in mathematics. Young people need to build competency and automaticity in procedures and processes. Often, the most efficient way to do this is through the teacher modelling the processes. However, repetition without understanding can result in reinforced misconceptions.

Take, for example, a learning intention of building automaticity in the addition of two-digit numbers using the vertical algorithm. The desire is to build fluency in the process of using the vertical algorithm is the focus. Student’s prior knowledge and understanding must be well-established and used in the process of building automaticity. Consider 53+29. Students should be able to ’sense check’ their answer so as to deduce that an answer of 712 cannot be correct.

In doing so, students in conjunction with the teacher are using efficient strategies to obtain answer. The journey, however, is scaffolded to make full use of prior understanding.
